Solar Hydrogen Vehicle

Principles of Engineering
November 1, 2018 - November 16, 2018

In the Solar Hydrogen Vehicle Project, a car using solar panels and hydrogen cells was designed, with the main goal of making a recommendation to the client on which configuration of electrical wiring and power sources would be the most efficient. With the collaboration of my team, I constructed the main chassis of the vehicle as well as the mountings for motors, power sources, and the breadboard. Throughout the iterative design process, we consciously made decisions in order to optimize the vehicle for speed. As opposed to using VEX steel parts, we prioritized working with plastic and aluminum components, to make the overall mass of the vehicle less. Since the vehicle was required to drive for exactly one meter, it was decided to sacrifice structural stability for reducing weight. After testing various configurations with series and parallel circuits and combinations of solar and hydrogen power sources, we recommended using two solar panels in series, to achieve higher voltage and greater efficiency. The results of testing and in depth analysis of our recommendation can be found in our team online documentation.

West Point Bridge Design

Principles of Engineering
March 7, 2019 - March 19, 2019

The objective of the West Point Bridge Design Project was to design a bridge that was cost efficient, specifically less than $300,000, and also had adequate stability. Initially, research was conducted on the various types of trusses used for industrial purposes. With my partner Gaurav Agarwal, we each built two unique trusses using the Bridge Designer 2016 Software, which allowed us to customize the material type, member lengths, cross-sectional area of members, and joint locations. Through the iterative design process, we tweaked the cross sectional areas and material types of the members to create the most cost effective bridge. From the four bridges constructed, we systematically evaluated each one based on stability, cost, and simplicity to choose the best overall bridge to choose as our final design solution. Details on the Decision Matrix and the four bridge designs were outlined in our project documentation. The final design solution, depicted to the right, features all carbon steel, the cheapest material available, as well as a pier, which extends upwards from the middle of the waterway. This bridge demonstrates an ideal balance between stability and cost-effectiveness, as it is cheaper than $300,000 and still maintains rigidity.

Android Studio App

AP Computer Science A
September 27, 2019 - October 9, 2019

The objective of the Android Studio App Project was to develop a prototype of a mobile application which would be useful in solving an everyday problem. Initially, our group, consisting of Derek X., Joshua B, and I, brainstormed a list of common problems that can be solved. We ultimately decided to create an app which would quickly do mathematical analysis on simple shapes. This app, named "No More Geometry" strives to provide struggling geometry students with support and help them perform simple calculations with ease and quickness. Through the iterative design process, we received feedback from other student groups to refine our concept and get new, creative ideas. In the end, the app featured a simple user interface which allowed users to input a single quantity of the given shape (perimeter, area, side length, radius, etc.) and the app would calculate the rest. More details of the design process can be found in our project documentation.
